# Question Guides — the top-journal bar for choosing & scoring research questions

*Reference for the **Generate questions** and **Evaluate questions** steps. Distilled from
the editorial statements and referee guidance of the top economics journals (AER, QJE, JPE,
Econometrica, REStud) and their applied siblings (AEJ: Applied, AEJ: Policy, AER: Insights),
plus the canonical referee essay (Berk–Harvey–Hirshleifer, JEP 2017), Sobel's Econometrica
editor musings, Bellemare's applied-paper guide, and Angrist–Pischke on credibility. This is
about the **research question**, not the writing — for prose, see `PAPER_GUIDES.md`.*

**The standard:** a question that could anchor a paper a **top economics journal** would
publish. Acceptance there runs ~6–8% overall; the binding constraint is **not correctness —
it is importance and contribution.** As Sobel (Econometrica) puts it, most rejections turn on
"a subjective evaluation of the marginal contribution of the paper rather than a logical
error." A clean, correct, narrow question is a *routine reject*.

---

## 1. The three tests every question must pass (Berk–Harvey–Hirshleifer)

An editor's decision "boils down" to three determinants. A question clears the bar only if it
plausibly passes **all three at once**:

1. **Broad interest** — "whether it addressed a question of sufficiently broad interest."
   Would an economist *outside* this subfield care about the answer? (The general-interest
   desk screen: "would a non-specialist care?") QJE's Katz: general interest is decoupled
   from the country/setting — it comes from "the quality of the ideas," not from a topic that
   merely looks broad.
2. **A sufficient leap over the literature** — "whether it made a sufficient leap over
   existing literature." Most submissions are *correct*; few make the leap. Beware "small
   wrinkles in existing analysis" (Arrow) — an incremental extension of a known result fails
   even if flawless.
3. **A correct, credibly identified analysis** — the answer must be obtainable with a design
   that "makes a case for causal interpretation" (Angrist–Pischke). Post-credibility-
   revolution, an applied question needs a believable identification path (experiment /
   natural experiment / IV / DiD / RDD, or a clean descriptive measurement when that is
   itself the contribution), with the key threat to identification answerable in the data.

**Calibrating heuristic (use this when scoring):** *"Flaws and all, would I have been pleased
to have written the paper this question leads to?"* If yes, it scores high. Importance
outranks polish — do not down-score an ambitious question for fixable flaws; only for flaws
that would **invalidate the contribution**, or for being merely correct-but-incremental.

---

## 2. What raises the ceiling (good-question signals)

- **A real "so what?"** — the answer changes priors people hold, informs a policy lever, or
  resolves a puzzle / tension in the literature. It completes "I wonder if…" or "It is
  interesting that…".
- **Names a contribution, and how.** Ideally ~3 contributions (Bellemare): better
  identification (internal validity), broader/new data or setting (external validity), and/or
  the **mechanism** the effect operates through. One contribution is enough *if it is
  important enough*.
- **New evidence + a mechanism**, not just a documented correlation. "Documenting an
  empirical regularity is typically not enough" — say *why* the result holds.
- **Focus.** Top journals reward "a single, relatively narrow research question answered
  well." For an AER:Insights-style result: one clean insight, self-contained, no "moving
  parts" needing long set-up.
- **Generalizes beyond one idiosyncratic setting** — or, if single-setting, the setting is
  itself important or uniquely identifying.
- **Feasible to answer well with attainable data and enough identifying variation** (this is
  what the question-preview probe checks — a high-interest question with no identifying
  variation in the available data should be scored down, not pursued).

## 3. Auto-low-score anti-patterns (what gets desk-rejected)

- **Contribution defined by technique, estimator, or dataset.** "0 × (fancy technique) = 0"
  (Bellemare). "What if I apply method M to X–Y?" or "What if I change an assumption in
  famous Model X?" are not questions. A new dataset for a stale question fails.
- **Merely correct but incremental** — a "small wrinkle"; the single most common reason
  correct papers are rejected.
- **Restating the topic as the contribution** — "this topic is well studied / important" is
  not a contribution; neither is restating the research question.
- **Narrow / field-only interest** — technically fine but of interest only inside one
  subfield (belongs in a field journal, not a top-5).
- **No credible identification path** — the central confound has no answer in any attainable
  data; the design cannot support the causal claim the question implies.
- **No identifying variation** — even an important, novel question dies if the data have
  near-zero variation in the treatment over the relevant window (the test_05 failure mode).
- **Out of scope** — wrong journal fit is the most common desk-reject trigger; an empirical-
  micro question for a theory venue (or vice versa) is dead on arrival.

## 4. Scope notes (where a question would land)

- **General-interest top-5 (AER, QJE, JPE, Econometrica, REStud):** broad significance +
  large leap. AER prizes "the importance of the questions it addresses, the quality of
  analysis, the innovation over the literature." Econometrica uniquely rewards deep
  theoretical/methodological contributions (narrow-but-rigorous is OK there).
- **AEJ: Applied:** empirical microeconomics — labor, development, health, education,
  empirical IO/finance/trade/behavioral. Pure theory is out of scope.
- **AEJ: Policy:** the common theme is "the role of economic policy in economic outcomes" —
  the question must connect to a policy lever.
- **AER: Insights:** one important insight conveyed succinctly; self-contained, "not teasers
  for the coming attractions in the appendices."

## 5. Scoring rubric — TIER 1 (top-5), 1–10, for the Evaluate-questions step

This is the **Tier-1** rubric (the general-interest top-5 bar). When the orchestrator asks you
to score at **Tier 2**, use the recalibrated anchors in §6 instead — the three tests and the
identification/feasibility bar are unchanged; only the *importance* and *leap* heights move.

Each scorer assigns **1–10** to a candidate, judging it as a *prospective top-5 paper* on the
three tests in §1 (weight them roughly equally; a question that fails any one test cannot score
above ~5). Three scorers sum to a **max of 30**; the pursue gate is **> 20** (≈ an average of 7
— "clearly clears broad-interest and a sufficient leap, with a credible, feasible identification
path"). Anchors:

- **9–10** — Broad cross-field interest; a clear, large leap over the literature; a credible
  identification path that is feasible with attainable data. "I'd be pleased to have written
  it." Plausible top-5.
- **7–8** — Important and genuinely novel; credible design; maybe single-contribution or
  field-leaning. Clears the bar (top field journal / strong top-5 candidate). **At/above the
  pursue line.**
- **5–6** — Interesting but the leap is modest, the interest is subfield-bound, or the
  identification is shaky/uncertain. Below the bar as posed; might be reframed.
- **3–4** — Incremental, technique-driven, or no credible identification / no identifying
  variation in attainable data. Desk-reject territory.
- **1–2** — Not a real contribution; restated topic; out of scope; infeasible.

Score against the **question preview** (novelty verdict from the lit-check, the design
skeleton, and the feasibility/power signal) — not the bare question. A high-interest question
with a failing feasibility probe should be scored down, because "good answers to good
questions" require both that the question is good *and* that it can actually be answered well.

---

## 6. The publication ladder & the Tier-2 re-score

There are two scoring tiers. A run scores **Tier 1 first**; if nothing clears the gate, it
**re-scores the same candidates at Tier 2** (one pass — no new questions). Pursue the top
question of whichever tier clears the gate. Only if **both** tiers fail is the result a genuine
"no viable paper" (and even then, report the best candidate and the tier it could reach with
better data).

**The ladder:**
- **Tier 1 — general-interest top-5:** AER, QJE, JPE, Econometrica, REStud. Broad significance
  + a large leap. (§5 anchors.)
- **Tier 2 — strong field / applied:** AEJ: Applied, AEJ: Economic Policy, AER: Insights,
  Journal of Human Resources, Journal of Public Economics, Journal of Labor Economics, JEEM,
  Review of Economics and Statistics. These are *real, selective* journals — a Tier-2 paper is
  a credibly-identified, honest contribution that matters to a field, not a weaker paper.

**What recalibrates at Tier 2 — and what does NOT.** This distinction is the whole point;
violating it turns the ladder into manufacturing:
- **RELAXES — importance / broad interest:** field-specific or policy-relevant interest is
  enough; the question need not grab economists outside its subfield.
- **RELAXES — the leap:** a solid incremental advance, a new setting or dataset, or a careful
  *measurement* contribution clears it; it need not be a large leap over the literature.
- **HOLDS AT FULL STRENGTH — identification, feasibility, honesty:** Tier 2 (especially AEJ:
  Applied) is, if anything, *more* identification-focused than the top-5. A question with no
  credible design, no identifying variation in attainable data, or an infeasible data
  requirement **fails at Tier 2 exactly as it fails at Tier 1.** Tier 2 is a lower *importance*
  bar, never a lower *rigor* bar. (Concretely: a weak-but-real IV on an important applied
  question can clear Tier 2; an HTE correlation with no instrument, a single-case "DiD," or an
  RD whose forcing variable doesn't exist cannot.)

**Tier-2 anchors (reset 1–10 scale; same > 20/30 gate):**
- **9–10** — A strong, well-identified applied/field contribution; clearly clears AEJ:Applied /
  a flagship field journal. Important to its field, credibly causal (or a genuinely valuable
  measurement), feasible.
- **7–8** — A solid, credibly-identified field paper; modest leap or field-bound interest, but
  the design is sound and feasible. **At/above the pursue line.**
- **5–6** — Interesting to a field but the contribution is thin, OR the identification is
  shaky/uncertain even by field standards. Below the Tier-2 bar.
- **3–4** — Incremental, technique-driven, or no credible/feasible identification. Fails Tier 2.
- **1–2** — Not a real contribution; out of scope; infeasible.

Note the asymmetry: importance can drop a question two tiers, but a **rigor or feasibility
failure caps the score at ~4 on BOTH tiers.** That is what keeps the fallback honest.
